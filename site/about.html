Occupations

The 516 occupations come from the COPS 2024–2033 summary CSV, which lists every NOC 2021 unit group with labour market projections. We exclude the "All Occupations" aggregate and all TEER / major-group subtotals, keeping only individual 5-digit unit groups.

485 of 516 occupations have employment figures. The remaining 31 are suppressed by Statistics Canada due to small sample sizes. Those 485 represent 20.1 million jobs — the full Canadian employed labour force.

Wage estimates

Statistics Canada's Labour Force Survey (LFS) median wage tables at the unit-group level are only publicly available up to 2013. We use Table 14-10-0417 (median hourly wages, Canada, all genders, age 15+) and scale forward to 2024 dollars:

Formula: hourly_2024 = hourly_2013 × 1.35 (≈ 2.8% per year compounding)
annual_2024 = round(hourly_2024 × 2000) (standard 40 hr/week, 50 weeks)

The 1.35 inflation factor is derived from Statistics Canada's Consumer Price Index: the CPI rose from 122.8 (2013) to 165.7 (2024 est.), a ratio of 1.349. Wages are assigned at the broad occupational group level — all unit groups within the same 2-digit NOC prefix share the same base wage. This is an approximation; individual occupations vary considerably within groups.

For NOC 2021 codes starting with 6–9, the TEER level is the second digit of the code. For codes 0–5, the TEER is the first digit. This corrects a common misreading of the code structure.

Labour market outlook

The 3-Year Employment Outlooks 2025–2027 XLSX contains ~44,000 rows — one per occupation × province/territory. There is no pre-computed national row. We derive a national outlook by taking the modal (most common) rating across all 13 regions for each occupation.

AI exposure scoring

Each occupation receives a score from 0 to 10 measuring how much AI is expected to reshape that occupation — both through direct automation and through productivity amplification (fewer workers needed per unit of output).

Scores are generated by GPT-4o-mini (OpenAI) reading a structured Markdown description of the occupation, including: NOC code, TEER level, major category, employment 2023, projected job openings 2024–2033, and labour market conditions. The model is instructed to respond with a single JSON object: {"exposure": N, "rationale": "..."}. Temperature is set to 0.2 for consistency.

Pipeline

Seven scripts, run in order, build everything from raw government files to the website:

What gets scraped from Job Bank

For the 462 occupations with a direct Job Bank profile, scrape_jobbank.py collects:

  • Canonical title — the most common job-posting title on Job Bank (e.g. "Shop Clerk" instead of the NOC label "Retail salespersons, visual merchandisers and related workers"). This is what appears in treemap boxes and tooltips.
  • Employment requirements — up to 3 bullet points from the Employment requirements section, sourced from the NOC description (e.g. "A bachelor's degree in computer science… is usually required."). Shown in the hover tooltip alongside the TEER-based education tier.

Known limitations

  • Wages are approximated. Wages come from 2013 LFS data scaled by a single inflation factor. Real wages vary significantly within each group, and some occupations have seen wage growth well above or below the average.
  • No unit-group wage data after 2013. Statistics Canada does not release LFS median wages at the 5-digit NOC unit-group level after 2013. More granular or recent wage data is behind a custom data request.
  • National outlook is a modal proxy. The 3-year outlook XLSX contains only provincial/territorial rows. We use the most common rating across all regions, which may not reflect national conditions accurately for occupations with strong regional variation (e.g., oil-patch trades).
  • AI exposure is an LLM opinion. Scores come from a language model reading a text description — they are not empirically measured automation probabilities. The model calibration anchors reduce variance, but individual scores should be interpreted as rough tiers, not precise values.
  • 31 occupations lack employment data. Statistics Canada suppresses figures for occupations with very small sample sizes. These occupations are shown in the treemap at a minimal size.
  • Job Bank URLs: 54 occupations use search fallback. The Job Bank's internal concordance database does not include NOC 2021 codes for 54 unit groups (primarily very new or restructured categories). Clicking those occupations opens a pre-filled Job Bank search instead of a direct profile page.
  • Employment year is 2023. The COPS dataset uses 2023 as the base employment year, not 2024 or 2025.
